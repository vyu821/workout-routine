Chest
Dumbbell Flat Bench Press
Pec major (sternal)
4 × 8–10
90s rest
Primary chest mass builder. Control the descent, pause briefly at the chest, drive up. Keep your shoulder blades pinched back into the bench throughout.
Incline Dumbbell Press
Pec major (clavicular)
3 × 10–12
75s rest
Set bench to 30–45°. Hits the upper chest that flat work misses. Keep elbows at about 70° to the torso, not flared wide.
Dumbbell Chest Fly
Pec major (stretch)Pec minor
3 × 12–15
60s rest
Flat bench. Arms slightly bent, lower wide until you feel a deep stretch, squeeze back together. Isolation move, go lighter than you think.
Push-Ups
CalisthenicsPec minor · Serratus
2 × Fail
60s rest
Finisher. Hands slightly wider than shoulder-width. Go to failure or do slow negatives (3 sec down). Great for serratus anterior and pec minor activation.
Shoulders
Seated DB Overhead Press
Anterior deltMedial delt
4 × 8–10
90s rest
Bench fully upright. Shoulder mass builder. Press to full extension, lower to ear level. Core tight throughout.
Dumbbell Lateral Raise
Medial delt
4 × 12–15
60s rest
The exercise that actually builds shoulder width. Lead with the elbow, slight forward tilt, raise to shoulder height. Avoid shrugging. Slow and controlled, not swung.
Dumbbell Front Raise
Anterior delt
3 × 12
60s rest
Alternating or both arms. Raise to shoulder height, no higher. Slight bend in elbow. Rounds out the front delt that pressing alone doesn't fully isolate.
Triceps
Dumbbell Skull Crushers
Long headMedial head
3 × 10–12
75s rest
Flat bench. Lower dumbbells to the sides of your head, elbows pointing up. The long head gets the best stretch in this position. Key for overall tricep size.
Overhead DB Extension
Long head (stretch)
3 × 12
60s rest
One dumbbell held with both hands overhead. Lower behind your head, elbows in tight. The stretch-loaded position on the long head is unmatched for growth.
Diamond / Close-Grip Push-Ups
CalisthenicsLateral headMedial head
2 × Fail
60s rest
Hands form a diamond or stay close. Elbows track back along your torso, not flared. Great finisher that hits the lateral head differently than DB work.
Tips Superset laterals with front raises to save time. Flat and incline press are your heaviest lifts so do those first. If you're short on time, drop one of the finisher push-up sets.

Back
Dumbbell Row
Latissimus dorsiMid traps
4 × 8–10
90s rest
Brace a knee and hand on the bench. Row the dumbbell to your hip, leading with the elbow. Full stretch at the bottom, full squeeze at the top. Primary lat builder.
Pull-Ups / Chin-Ups
CalisthenicsLats (width)Biceps
3 × Fail
90s rest
Pull-ups (overhand) bias lats more; chin-ups (underhand) bring in more biceps. Go to failure every set. Even 3–5 reps per set adds up.
Incline Prone DB Row
Mid / lower trapsRhomboids
3 × 12
75s rest
Set bench to 45°, lie chest-down, dumbbells hanging. Row both up, elbows flared to 90°. Excellent for postural muscles and upper back thickness standard rows miss.
Dumbbell Deadlift
Erector spinaeLower back
3 × 10
90s rest
Dumbbells at your sides, hip hinge (not a squat), flat back throughout. Push the floor away on the way up. Builds spinal erectors and teaches the hinge pattern.
Biceps
Dumbbell Curl
Biceps brachii (short head)
3 × 10–12
60s rest
Standing, alternating. Supinate (rotate) the wrist as you curl up. Full range of motion, no swinging. Primary bicep builder targeting the short head and outer peak.
Incline Dumbbell Curl
Biceps brachii (long head)
3 × 10–12
60s rest
Bench at 45–60°, lean back, arms hang fully behind you. Puts the long head in a deep stretch standing curls can't replicate. Slower and more controlled.
Hammer Curl
BrachialisBrachioradialis
3 × 12
60s rest
Neutral (thumbs-up) grip throughout. The brachialis sits beneath the bicep and, when developed, pushes the bicep up making arms look thicker. Also hits brachioradialis.
Concentration Curl
Biceps (short head)Peak isolation
2 × 12 /side
45s rest
Seated, elbow braced against the inside of your thigh, curl up. Eliminates all momentum. Emphasizes the short head and the peak. Best used as a finisher after heavier curl work.
Forearms
Zottman Curl
BrachioradialisBiceps brachiiBrachialis
3 × 10
60s rest
Curl up supinated (palms up), rotate to pronated (palms down) at the top, lower slowly. Trains all three elbow flexors through their strongest positions in one movement.
Reverse Curl
BrachioradialisExtensor carpi
3 × 10–12
60s rest
Overhand (pronated) grip, curl up the same as a normal curl. Brachioradialis does most of the work. Also hits the wrist extensors, important for elbow health.
Wrist Curl (Supinated)
Flexor carpi radialisFlexor carpi ulnaris
3 × 15–20
30s rest
Forearms on thighs or bench edge, palms up. Curl the wrist up and lower slowly. Builds the inner forearm belly. Go light, this is pure isolation.
Wrist Curl (Pronated)
Extensor carpi radialisExtensor carpi ulnaris
3 × 15–20
30s rest
Same setup but palms down. Extend the wrist up against gravity. The most neglected forearm muscles, and a common source of lateral elbow pain when weak. Superset directly with the supinated version.
Rear Delts
Bent-Over Reverse Fly
Posterior deltLower traps
4 × 12–15
60s rest
Hinge forward to ~45°, arms hanging. Raise arms out to the sides with a slight bend at the elbow, pinch the shoulder blades. Rear delts are almost always undertrained and matter a lot for shoulder health and posture.
Face Pull (floor variation)
Posterior deltRotator cuff
3 × 15
60s rest
No cable? Lie on the bench face-down with light dumbbells, arms hanging. Row the weights up and back, externally rotating at the top. Crucial for shoulder health and rotator cuff longevity.
Tips You don't need all the bicep variations every session. Rotate: one session do curls + incline curls, another do Zottmans + concentration curls. Always superset the two wrist curl variations back to back. Reverse curls can replace hammer curls on alternating weeks.

Dynamic Warmup (do before lifting)
Seated Heel-to-Hip Switch
Hip internal rotationHip external rotation
10 /side
flow
Sit on the floor, legs extended. Alternate bending one knee and dropping the foot to the side of the hip (Z-sit). Switch sides. Wakes up hip rotation, often the root cause of knee tracking issues in squats.
90/90 Hip Stretch
Hip external rotationPiriformis
60s /side
flow
Sit with both knees bent at 90°, one in front and one to the side. Sit tall and breathe into the stretch. Opens the hip capsule in a way standing stretches don't reach.
Active Straight Leg Raise (ASLR)
Hamstring flexibilityHip flexor activation
10 /side
flow
Lie on your back, legs extended. Actively raise one leg as high as you can with a straight knee, keeping the other leg pressed to the floor. Active, not passive; you're using your hip flexors to lift.
90/90 to Hip Lift
Glute activationHip extension
8 /side
flow
Start in 90/90. Press through the front foot and drive your hips up into full extension. Lower back down. Primes the glutes and hip extensors before you load them with squats and RDLs.
Kneeling Hip Flexor Lunge
IliopsoasRectus femoris
60s /side
flow
Half-kneeling, back knee on the floor, front foot forward. Drive hips forward gently until you feel the front of the back hip stretch. Squeeze the glute of the back leg to deepen it. Tight hip flexors are the biggest limiter of squat depth.
Deep Squat Hold (Asian Squat)
Ankle mobilityHip flexion
2 × 30–60s
flow
Feet shoulder-width, toes slightly out. Squat all the way down and hold, heels flat if possible. Grab a doorframe or hold a light dumbbell as a counterbalance if needed. Mobility test and warmup in one.
Deep Lateral Lunge
AdductorsHip abductors
8 /side
flow
Wide stance, shift weight to one side and sink into a deep side lunge, other leg straight. Inside of both feet stay flat. Alternate sides. Opens the groin and adductors, which are active in squats but rarely warmed up.
Quads
Goblet Squat
Quad (rectus femoris)VMO
4 × 10–12
90s rest
Hold one dumbbell vertically at your chest. Upright torso, wide stance, squat deep. The goblet position keeps your torso more vertical than a regular DB squat, maximizing quad load and knee tracking.
Bulgarian Split Squat
Quad (vastus lateralis)Glutes
3 × 8–10 /side
90s rest
Rear foot elevated on the bench, dumbbells at sides. Lower the back knee toward the floor. Brutal but one of the best single-leg exercises you can do. Glute involvement depends on your forward lean.
Dumbbell Step-Up
VMORectus femoris
3 × 10 /side
75s rest
Use the bench as the step, dumbbells at sides. Drive through the heel of your lead foot. Don't push off with the back leg. The VMO (teardrop) loves this movement.
Cossack Squat
Vastus medialis (VMO)AdductorsGlutes
3 × 8 /side
75s rest
Wide stance, squat deeply to one side while the other leg stays fully extended with toes pointing up. Trains inner quad and adductors regular squats miss. Hold a dumbbell at chest for counterbalance.
Hamstrings
Romanian Deadlift (RDL)
Biceps femorisSemitendinosus
4 × 10–12
90s rest
Dumbbells in front of thighs, slight knee bend. Hinge at the hips, lower along your legs until you feel a deep hamstring stretch. Drive hips forward to come back up. The best hamstring exercise you can do with dumbbells.
Nordic Hamstring Curl
CalisthenicsSemimembranosus
3 × 4–6
90s rest
Anchor feet under the bench. Kneel tall, then lower your torso to the floor using only your hamstrings, as slowly as possible. Catch yourself with hands, push back up. One of the highest-value exercises for hamstring strength and injury prevention.
Seated Good Morning
Erector spinaeSemimembranosusBiceps femoris
3 × 12
75s rest
Sit on the bench edge, hold a dumbbell behind your head or hug it at your chest. Hinge forward with a flat back until you feel a hamstring stretch, then drive back up. The seated position limits hip flexor involvement in a way standing RDLs don't.
Glutes
Dumbbell Hip Thrust
Gluteus maximus
4 × 12–15
75s rest
Upper back on the bench, dumbbell on your hips. Drive through your heels, squeeze your glutes hard at the top, hold 1 second. The glute max is the largest muscle in the body and often undertrained even in people who squat regularly.
Dumbbell Sumo Squat
Gluteus mediusInner quad
3 × 12
75s rest
Wide stance, toes out ~45°, one dumbbell hanging between your legs. Lower deep. The wide stance shifts work to the glute medius (outer glute) and inner quad that standard squats miss.
Calves & Tibialis
Standing Dumbbell Calf Raise
Gastrocnemius
4 × 15–20
45s rest
Hold dumbbells, raise on the balls of your feet, squeeze at the top, lower slowly. Toes forward. The gastroc is the visible two-headed calf muscle trained best with the knee extended.
Seated Calf Raise (bench)
Soleus
3 × 15–20
45s rest
Sit on the bench, dumbbell balanced on knee, raise up. Knees bent shifts load to the soleus, the deeper calf muscle that gives the calf its overall thickness from the front.
Tibialis Raise
CalisthenicsTibialis anterior
3 × 20–25
30s rest
Back against a wall, heels about 6 inches from the baseboard. Lift toes and forefoot off the ground repeatedly. The tibialis anterior is the direct antagonist to the calf, almost universally undertrained, and key for ankle stability and shin splint prevention.
Tips Do the full warmup before touching a weight; it takes about 8 minutes and makes a real difference in squat depth and knee tracking. Keep the heaviest compounds first: goblet squat, then RDL. Superset standing and seated calf raises back to back, then do tibialis raises. Nordic curls are hard, even 3 slow negatives per set counts.

Wall-Anchored Circuit (run as a sequence)
Wall Feet Hollow Body Crunch
CalisthenicsRectus abdominisTVA
3 × 15
flow
Lie on your back, feet flat on the wall, knees at 90°. Slide your feet up the wall by pressing into it and crunching your torso up simultaneously. The wall locks your lower body in place so the abs do all the work, no hip flexor cheating.
Heel Taps
CalisthenicsExternal obliquesRectus abdominis (lower)
3 × 20
flow
Same wall-anchored position. Shoulders slightly off the floor. Reach side to side, alternating touching each heel with the same-side hand. The lateral reach fires the obliques while the lower abs hold pelvic position.
Alternating Elbow-to-Knee
CalisthenicsExternal obliquesRectus abdominis
3 × 20
flow
Feet off the wall. Hands behind your head, bring the opposite elbow toward the opposite knee while extending the other leg. Slow and controlled, feel the rotation. One of the highest-activation ab exercises in EMG research.
Double Crunch
CalisthenicsRectus abdominis (upper + lower)
3 × 12
45s rest
Bring both elbows and both knees together at the same time in the middle. Upper body crunches up while the knees draw in simultaneously. Hits the full length of the rectus abdominis at once.
Anti-Extension (core stability)
Plank
CalisthenicsTransverse abdominisDeep core
3 × 30–60s
45s rest
Forearms or hands, straight line from head to heels. Squeeze everything: glutes, abs, quads. Breathing should stay controlled. The TVA is your internal weight belt and the foundation of all core work.
Ab Wheel / Dumbbell Rollout
CalisthenicsRectus abdominisTVA
3 × 8–12
60s rest
Hold a dumbbell on the floor and roll it out as far as you can while keeping a neutral spine. Roll it back without letting your hips sag. Arguably the single best ab exercise for building thickness.
Flexion
Weighted Crunch
CalisthenicsRectus abdominis (upper)
3 × 15–20
45s rest
Hold a dumbbell at your chest. Curl your shoulders off the floor, exhale hard at the top. Slow and intentional reps beat high-rep sloppy ones.
Reverse Crunch
CalisthenicsRectus abdominis (lower)
3 × 12–15
45s rest
Lie flat, bring your knees to your chest and curl your hips off the floor. Lower with control. The lower rectus abdominis is notoriously hard to isolate and this is one of the few moves that genuinely does it.
Rotation & Anti-Rotation (obliques)
Dumbbell Russian Twist
External obliquesInternal obliques
3 × 16–20
45s rest
Seated, torso leaned back slightly, feet up or down. Hold one dumbbell and rotate side to side, touching the weight to the floor. Rotate from your torso, not just your arms.
Side Plank
CalisthenicsInternal obliquesQuadratus lumborum
3 × 30s /side
30s rest
Forearm on the floor, body in a straight line, hips elevated. Add a dumbbell on the hip for extra load. Trains the obliques and QL as stabilizers, which matters for spine health and lateral strength.
When to do Abs Tack this on at the end of any session. Run the wall-anchored circuit as one block with minimal rest, then pick 2–3 from the remaining sections. Takes about 15–20 minutes. Abs recover fast, 3–4 sessions per week is fine. Rotate exercises so every part of the core gets hit across the week.
